###History
The Honda Civic is a series of automobiles manufactured by Honda since 1972. As of 2023, the Civic is positioned between the Honda Fit/City and Honda Accord in Honda's global passenger car lineup. It is one of the best-selling automobiles in history, with over 27 million units sold through 2021. Initially known for fuel efficiency, reliability, and environmental friendliness, later iterations became recognized for performance and sportiness, especially the Civic Si, SiR, and Type R versions. It also served as the basis for models like the Honda CR-X, CR-X del Sol, Prelude, Civic Shuttle, Orthia, and CR-V.

###First Generation (1972–1979)
Introduced on July 11, 1972 (sold as a 1973 model). Equipped with a 1,169 cc four-cylinder water-cooled engine, front disc brakes, reclining vinyl bucket seats, simulated wood dashboard trim, optional air conditioning and AM/FM radio. Available as two- or four-door fastback sedan, three- and five-door hatchback, and five-door station wagon. CVCC engine introduced in December 1973, allowing compliance with 1975 EPA emission standards without a catalytic converter. The platform was expanded into the Honda Accord in 1976.

###	Second Generation (1979–1983)
Introduced in June 1979 as a 1980 model. Larger and more angular with more engine power. All engines used CVCC technology with lean-burn swirl and three valves per cylinder. Engine options: 1,335 cc and 1,488 cc, power varied by market. Transmission options: four-speed manual, five-speed manual, and two-speed semi-automatic “Hondamatic.” Body styles: three-door hatchback, four-door sedan, five-door hatchback, five-door wagon.

###Third Generation (1983–1987)
Released in September 1983 for the 1984 model year. Five-door “shuttle wagon” and CRX coupe introduced. Engines: 1.5 L CVCC (76 hp) and 1.6 L DOHC ZC (130 hp in Japanese Si). U.S. Si variant: 3-door hatchback and CRX Si with 91 hp. 4WD system called “Realtime” introduced with viscous coupler and later upgraded in 1987. Manual transmission featured synchronized 6th “SL” gear.

###Fourth Generation (1987–1991)
Introduced in September 1987. Larger dimensions, lower hood line. Japanese SiR featured 1.6 L DOHC VTEC B16A engine. U.S. models had electronic fuel injection. Fully independent rear suspension introduced. CRX remained part of Civic family. Variants in South Africa built as Ballade. First 800 cars in Canada were Special Edition (SE) models with white side molding, dual mirrors, blue dashboard stripe, interior upgrades, and chrome-tipped exhaust.

###Fifth Generation (1991–1995)
Introduced in September 1991 for the 1992 model year. Increased dimensions, aerodynamic styling. Wagon variant limited to Japan. VX replaced HF as most fuel-efficient model (EPA 48/55 mpg). North American Si: 1.6 L SOHC VTEC; VX: VTEC-E. Japanese Si: 1.6 L DOHC non-VTEC D16A9. Special Japanese SiR and international variants continued sporty tradition. South Africa: Ballade 180i with B18B4 engine. New body style: Civic Coupe based on Civic Ferio sedan.

###Sixth Generation (1995–2000)
Introduced September 1995 for 1996 model year. Updated styling, first natural gas Civic (GX). U.S. trims: CX, DX, EX, EXR, HX, LX; Canada: SE, Si. Base engines: 1.6 L 4-cylinder SOHC. U.S. Si and Canadian SiR: 1.6 L DOHC VTEC B16A2 (160 hp). Europe: DOHC 1.6 VTi hatchback/sedan, 1.8 L Domani liftback/estate. Acura EL in Canada based on Civic.

###Seventh Generation (2000–2005)
Released September 2000 for 2001 model year. Interior space improved with flat rear floor; Civic moved to compact class. Front suspension switched to MacPherson strut. Four trims: DX, LX, EX, HX. North America: coupe and sedan, Si offered as three-door hatchback. First Civic Hybrid introduced with 1.3 L engine.

###Eighth Generation (2005–2011)
Released September 2005 for 2006 model year. Two platforms: sedan/coupe and European hatchback (simpler rear suspension from Fit). Hatchback: three- and five-door. Si and Type R continued; Japanese and European Type R mechanically different. U.S.: Mugen-tuned Civic Si 4-door available. Canada: Acura CSX introduced. By 2006: 16.5 million Civics sold worldwide, 7.3 million in the U.S.

###Ninth Generation (2011–2015)
Sedan, coupe, hatchback, station wagon (Civic Tourer) in Europe. Hatchback base for Civic Type R FK2 (2015). U.S. sales began April 2011 for 2012 model year. Developed during 2008 financial crisis: focus on affordability and fuel efficiency. Hybrid sedan: 1.5 L i-VTEC, 67 kW (90 hp), 132 Nm torque, 5.3 L/100 km combined fuel economy. Ninth-generation Civic not sold in Japan except 750-unit Type R (2015).

###Tenth Generation (2015–2021)
All-new global compact platform. Sedan unveiled September 2015 for 2016 model year. Fastback exterior, rear C-pillar flowing into tailgate. Body styles: sedan, coupe, five-door hatchback. Performance: Si and Type R. Hatchback reintroduced to North America for first time since 2000; first Type R sold in region. Driver Information Interface with 7-inch LCD screen optional. Analog tachometer surrounding digital speedometer in some trims.

###Eleventh Generation (2021–Present)
Sedan prototype revealed November 2020, production in June 2021. Body styles: sedan and liftback (Civic Hatchback). North America sales June 2021; Southeast Asia August; Japan/China September; Australia/New Zealand December; Pakistan March 2022; Europe late 2022. No coupe offered due to declining sales; sedan not sold in Japan, Europe, or Australia. Liftback unveiled June 23, 2021.

###International Marketing and Derivatives
Different model names by market: Japan – hatchback “Civic,” sedan called Civic Ferio (5th–7th generations). Sixth-generation sedan sold as Integra SJ. Europe/US: generic “Civic,” coupe branded “Civic Coupe.” Civic Shuttle (1984–1991), Ballade in South Africa. Models derived from Civic platform: Prelude, Ballade, CR-X, Quint, Concerto, Domani, CR-X Del Sol, Integra, CR-V. Rover and Triumph sold Civic-based models. Brazilian production began 1997 (6th gen sedan) with flex-fuel adaptations (E20–E25, ethanol up to E100).

###Safety
U.S.: NHTSA frontal crash ratings vary by year. Eighth-generation sedan highly rated by NHTSA and IIHS; coupe rated “acceptable” for side impact. Australia: Used Car Safety Ratings 1984–2005; ANCAP ratings from 2007 onwards.

###Modifications and Enthusiast Community
Civic popular for modification and customization. Parts availability and interchangeability allow easy engine swaps and upgrades.

###Awards
International Car of the Year 2005 (EX), Car of the Year Japan 1972–1974, European Car of the Year 1973 (3rd), U.S. Road Test 1974 Car of the Year. Motor Trend Import Car of the Year 1980, 2006 Car of the Year. Canadian Car of the Year awards, North American Car of the Year, Auto Esporte 2006, South African Car of the Year 2007, Kelley Blue Book Best Buy 2020, Scottish Car of the Year 2022.

###Racing
Civic used in touring car, WTCC, BTCC, TCR, rally, endurance, and other motorsport series. Won WTCC 2013 Manufacturers Championship, multiple TCR global titles (2019, 2020, 2024). Holds Nürburgring front-wheel-drive lap record.
